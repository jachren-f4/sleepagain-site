Night Owls and Evolutionary Sleep: Why Your Brain Wants You Awake at 1am

By Joakim Achrén · March 15, 2026

I spent years thinking something was wrong with me. Every productivity guru I followed woke up at 5am. Every founder interview I consumed made it sound like dawn was the price of admission to success. So I forced myself into early alarms, morning workouts, and pre-sunrise deep work.

It didn't work. I was groggy until 10am. My best thinking happened after lunch. I did my clearest writing in the evening when the house was quiet. For a long time, I assumed this meant I lacked discipline. It took me years to learn the real explanation: my brain is wired for evening alertness, and that wiring has been around for tens of thousands of years.

The Sentinel Hypothesis

In 2017, a team of researchers led by David Samson studied the Hadza, a group of hunter-gatherers in Tanzania who live in a way that closely resembles how early humans likely lived. They tracked the sleep patterns of 33 individuals over 20 days and found something striking. At almost no point during the night was the entire group asleep at the same time. Out of over 200 hours of observation, there were only 18 minutes when every person was simultaneously sleeping.

This wasn't coordination. Nobody was assigned a watch shift. The group's natural variation in sleep timing, their different chronotypes, created an automatic sentry system. The early risers covered dawn. The night owls covered the late hours. The group survived because its members were biologically staggered.

Samson called this the sentinel hypothesis. The idea is that evolution didn't just tolerate chronotype diversity. It selected for it. Groups with a wider spread of sleep-wake times had better overnight vigilance and were less vulnerable to predators. The night owl wasn't a liability. They were the one keeping everyone alive while the early birds slept.

The Wolf Chronotype

Sleep researcher Michael Breus categorized chronotypes into four animals: Lion, Bear, Wolf, and Dolphin. Wolves are the evening types. They struggle in the early morning, hit their cognitive stride in the afternoon, and do their most creative work after 4pm. Their melatonin release starts later, which means their biology is literally on a delayed schedule compared to the majority.

I'm a Wolf. When I accepted that instead of fighting it, everything shifted. I moved deep work to late morning. I pushed meetings to the afternoon. I stopped trying to write strategy documents at 7am when my brain was still warming up. The schedule change was maybe 90 minutes in either direction. But the quality of my output changed significantly.

Around 20% of the population are clear evening types. Another 20% are clear morning types (Lions). The remaining 60% sit in the middle (Bears), following roughly the solar cycle. There is also a fourth type, the Dolphin, characterized by irregular and often anxious sleep. These categories aren't rigid, but most people recognize themselves in one of them quickly.

It's Genetic, Not a Character Flaw

Your chronotype is shaped by genetics. One of the key drivers is a gene called PER3. People with a longer variant tend to be morning types. Those with a shorter variant lean toward evening. A 2019 study of nearly 700,000 people identified over 350 genetic variants associated with chronotype. This isn't a personality quiz result. It's physiology written into your DNA.

Chronotypes also shift across your lifetime. Babies wake at dawn. Teenagers drift strongly toward evening, which is why your 15-year-old can't get out of bed at 7am. That's not rebellion. It's biology. Most people settle into their adult pattern around age 23 or 24. Then in the mid-50s, the clock shifts earlier again.

For founders in their 30s and 40s, this means the chronotype you have now is likely the one you'll be working with for the next two decades. It's worth learning where you fall instead of spending those years fighting it.

Why This Matters for Founders

A University of Minnesota study found that when students started class just one hour later, their grades improved by a full letter grade. A larger study of roughly 800 university students, known as the Synchrony Effect, showed the same pattern. When schedules aligned with individual chronotypes, performance improved across the board. Morning types didn't outperform evening types because of higher IQ or more discipline. They outperformed because the system was built around their rhythm.

As founders, we have an advantage most people don't. We can design our own schedules. Nobody is forcing us to start at 8am. But that advantage is useless if we don't know what we're designing around. If you're a Wolf running a Lion's schedule, you're leaving cognitive performance on the table every morning.

The founder who wakes at 5am isn't more disciplined than the one who wakes at 8am. They're biologically different. Your chronotype is not something to overcome. It's something to build around. Identify your peak alertness window, protect it for your most demanding cognitive work, and stop feeling guilty about when that window happens to fall.

Evolution didn't make night owls by accident. It made them because the group needed someone awake at 1am. That instinct is still running. The least you can do is stop apologizing for it and start using it.
