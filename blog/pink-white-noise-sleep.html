Pink Noise, White Noise, and Sleep: What Actually Works

By Joakim Achrén · March 15, 2026

An ARC reader recently asked why I didn't cover pink noise and white noise in the book. The honest answer is that my own results were inconsistent, and the research is mixed enough that I didn't feel comfortable presenting it as a proven strategy. But the topic deserves a longer look, especially because so many founders swear by it. Here's what I've found.

The Three Types

White noise contains all frequencies at equal intensity. It sounds like static, a TV tuned to a dead channel, or a rushing waterfall up close. It's the harshest of the three and the most commonly used in sleep machines.

Pink noise reduces the higher frequencies and emphasizes the lower ones. It sounds softer and deeper. Think steady rainfall, wind through trees, or a river at a distance. Most people find it more natural and less fatiguing than white noise.

Brown noise goes further, cutting even more high frequency content. It's a deep rumble, like distant thunder or a strong wind against a building. It has gained a following online, especially among people with ADHD who report it helps them focus and sleep. The research on brown noise specifically is still thin, but the anecdotal reports are consistent enough to be worth noting.

What the Research Says

The strongest evidence is for pink noise and deep sleep. A 2013 study published in Neuron by Ngo et al. found that pink noise timed to match the brain's slow-wave oscillations during deep sleep enhanced memory consolidation in young adults. A 2017 follow-up by Papalambros et al. in Frontiers in Human Neuroscience showed a similar effect in older adults, with participants performing 25 to 30% better on memory tests the next morning when exposed to pink noise during sleep.

These are real results. But there are caveats. Both studies used carefully timed acoustic stimulation, not just continuous background noise from a phone app. The pink noise was delivered in precise bursts synchronized to the brain's slow waves using real-time EEG monitoring. A pink noise app playing all night is a fundamentally different intervention than what was tested in the lab.

White noise has more research behind it as a masking tool. A 2005 study in Sleep Medicine found that ICU patients fell asleep faster and had fewer awakenings when white noise was playing, compared to the normal hospital sound environment. The mechanism here is straightforward: consistent background sound masks sudden noises like car alarms, doors, or a partner moving in bed. Your brain is less likely to register a disruption when the baseline sound level is steady.

A 2021 meta-analysis in Sleep Medicine Reviews looked at 38 studies on continuous noise for sleep and found that the evidence was, in their words, low quality overall. Some studies showed benefits. Others showed no effect. A few showed that white noise actually fragmented sleep in some participants. The authors concluded that more rigorous research is needed before noise machines can be recommended as a standard clinical tool.

My Own Experience

I tried white noise first. I used a dedicated machine, not a phone, and ran it at low volume on the other side of the bedroom. For the first week, I noticed I was falling asleep faster. My mind seemed to settle more quickly, probably because the steady hiss covered the small sounds of the house that usually grabbed my attention.

After about two weeks, the effect faded. I started noticing the noise itself. It became another thing in the room rather than a background layer. I switched to pink noise and found it more pleasant but less effective at masking sounds. Brown noise through headphones was interesting for 20 minutes of pre-sleep relaxation, but wearing headphones all night wasn't practical.

I eventually went back to earplugs and silence. For me, the gains from noise machines didn't compare to simply blocking sound at the source. But I know several founders who use brown noise every night and say they can't sleep without it. Individual variation matters here more than in almost any other sleep intervention I've explored.

Practical Recommendations

If you want to try noise for sleep, here's what I'd suggest based on the research and my own testing.

Start with pink noise at low volume. It's the best-studied type for sleep quality, and most people find it more tolerable than white noise for all-night use. Steady rain or gentle wind recordings work well. Keep the volume low enough that you have to listen for it. If it's loud enough to be obvious, it's too loud.

Use a dedicated device, not your phone. Having your phone in the bedroom introduces other problems: the temptation to check it, notification sounds, blue light if you pick it up. A simple noise machine or a small Bluetooth speaker running a looped track avoids all of that.

Give it at least 5 nights before deciding. The first night or two can feel unusual. Your brain needs time to stop treating the sound as a new stimulus and start treating it as background.

If your main problem is sudden noise disruptions, a partner who snores, city traffic, early-morning construction, white noise is probably more effective than pink noise because it covers a wider frequency range. If your main problem is a racing mind at bedtime, pink or brown noise may help by giving your brain something neutral to anchor to instead of your thoughts.

And if none of it works, that's fine. Good earplugs and a dark room still beat any sound machine. The fundamentals of sleep hygiene, consistent timing, cool temperature, darkness, and a calm mind, will always matter more than what's playing in the background.
